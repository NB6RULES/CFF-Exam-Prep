Fanno Flow — Adiabatic Duct with Friction
3.1
Write the governing equations and assumptions for Fanno flow. Derive the Fanno line equation in h-s coordinates.
Module 3🔥 All Papers3–4 Marks
›
Assumptions of Fanno Flow
  • 1D steady flow in a constant area duct
  • Adiabatic (no heat transfer — Q = 0)
  • Perfect gas with constant γ and Cₚ
  • Friction force exists (wall shear stress τ_w ≠ 0)
  • No body forces, no work done
Governing Equations
Continuity: ρV = G = constant (G = mass flux = ṁ/A) Energy: h + V²/2 = h₀ = constant (adiabatic) Momentum: p + τ_w × P/A × dx = ... (friction term) Equation of state: p = ρRT, h = CₚT
Fanno Line in h-s Coordinates

The Fanno line is the locus of all possible states for a given mass flux G and stagnation enthalpy h₀ in an adiabatic constant-area duct.

From energy: h = h₀ - V²/2 From continuity: V = G/ρ Entropy as function of h: s = Cᵥ ln(T) + R ln(v) + const where v = 1/ρ Using T = h/Cₚ, v = RT/p, and p from momentum continuity: → The Fanno line in (h, s) space is a curved line Key feature: the line has a MAXIMUM entropy point at M = 1. Upper branch (above max): subsonic flow (M < 1) Lower branch (below max): supersonic flow (M > 1) Friction always drives flow TOWARD M = 1 (toward max entropy). Both subsonic and supersonic flows approach M=1 with friction.
Direction of Flow in Fanno Line
  • Subsonic (upper branch): Friction increases entropy → moves right along upper branch toward M=1. Velocity increases toward sonic.
  • Supersonic (lower branch): Friction increases entropy → moves right along lower branch toward M=1. Velocity decreases toward sonic.
  • Choking: M=1 is reached at the end of the duct — further duct length not possible without changing inlet conditions.
📝 Draw the Fanno line: a curve in h-s space. Mark M=1 at the nose (max entropy). Upper branch = subsonic, lower = supersonic. Draw arrows showing direction of increasing friction (moving toward M=1 nose).

Fanno Parameter — 4fL*/D

The Fanno parameter relates the current Mach number M to the maximum duct length from that point to choking (L*). f = Fanning friction factor, D = diameter:

4fL*/D = (1-M²)/(γM²) + (γ+1)/(2γ) × ln[(γ+1)M²/(2+(γ-1)M²)] For air (γ=1.4): 4fL*/D = (1-M²)/(1.4M²) + 1.2 × ln[2.4M²/(2+0.4M²)] To find duct length between sections 1 and 2: 4f(L₁-L₂)/D = (4fL*/D)₁ - (4fL*/D)₂ (values at M₁ and M₂ from Fanno tables)
Variation of All Flow Properties Along Fanno Duct
All properties relative to critical (*) conditions (at M=1): T/T* = (γ+1) / [2(1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)] = (γ+1)/2 × 1/(1+0.2M²) [air] p/p* = (1/M) × √[(γ+1)/(2+(γ-1)M²)] = (1/M) × √[(γ+1)M²T/((γ+1)T)] simplified = (1/M) × √[T*/T] further simplified ρ/ρ* = (1/M) × √[(2+(γ-1)M²)/(γ+1)] = p*/p × T/T* V/V* = M × √[T/T*] (since V=Ma and a=√(γRT)) p₀/p₀* = (1/M) × [(2/(γ+1)) × (1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)]^((γ+1)/2(γ-1)) For air (γ=1.4): p₀/p₀* = (1/M)[(1+0.2M²)/1.2]^3
Property Trends Along Fanno Duct
Subsonic Fanno (M<1 → M=1)
  • V increases (accelerates)
  • M increases toward 1
  • p decreases
  • T decreases
  • p₀ decreases (friction loss)
  • s increases
Supersonic Fanno (M>1 → M=1)
  • V decreases (decelerates)
  • M decreases toward 1
  • p increases
  • T increases
  • p₀ decreases (friction loss)
  • s increases
💡 In all Fanno problems: (1) find M₁ from given conditions, (2) look up 4fL*/D for M₁ from Fanno tables, (3) use 4fL/D = (4fL*/D)₁ - (4fL*/D)₂ to find M₂, (4) use ratio tables for all other properties.
3.3
Explain friction choking in Fanno flow. What happens when duct length exceeds maximum length?
Module 3🔥 Apr 2023 & Sept 20243 Marks
›
Friction Choking

In Fanno flow, friction always drives the Mach number toward M = 1 (choking condition). The maximum duct length L_max is the length at which M reaches exactly 1 at the exit.

Maximum duct length from entry to choked exit: L_max = D/(4f) × (4fL*/D)₁ [where (4fL*/D)₁ is the Fanno parameter at inlet M₁]
What Happens if L > L_max?
Subsonic inlet (M₁<1)
  • If L > L_max: mass flow rate DECREASES
  • Inlet conditions readjust to lower M₁
  • Exit remains choked at M=1
  • Effective: duct is too long → less flow enters
Supersonic inlet (M₁>1)
  • If L > L_max: a normal shock forms in duct
  • Shock converts flow to subsonic
  • Flow continues as subsonic Fanno to exit at M=1
  • Normal shock position adjusts with L
Rayleigh Flow — Frictionless Duct with Heat Transfer
3.4
Write governing equations of Rayleigh flow. Derive the Rayleigh line equation and plot it on a p-v diagram.
Module 3🔥 All Papers3–4 Marks
›
Assumptions of Rayleigh Flow
  • Constant area duct
  • Frictionless (f = 0, no wall friction)
  • Heat transfer present (Q ≠ 0) — the mechanism driving flow change
  • Steady 1D flow, perfect gas
Governing Equations
Continuity: ρV = G = constant Momentum: p + ρV² = p + G×V = constant ← (no friction!) → p + ρV² = p₁ + ρ₁V₁² Energy: h₀ changes (heat added/removed) h₀₂ - h₀₁ = q (heat added per unit mass)
Rayleigh Line in p-v Coordinates

From continuity: V = Gv (v = specific volume = 1/ρ). Substituting into momentum:

p + G²v = constant = p₁ + G²v₁ ∴ p = (p₁ + G²v₁) - G²v This is a STRAIGHT LINE with slope = -G² in (p, v) space. The Rayleigh line is a straight line with NEGATIVE slope on the p-v diagram. Maximum entropy on the Rayleigh line occurs at M = 1. Upper portion (high p, low v) = subsonic Lower portion (low p, high v) = supersonic
Rayleigh Line vs Fanno Line

Both lines pass through the same state points in a duct. Where they intersect: two possible states connected by a normal shock (the Rankine-Hugoniot solution).

📝 Draw p-v diagram: Rayleigh line as a straight line with negative slope. Mark M=1 at maximum entropy point (maximum T on upper intersection with isentropic). Mark subsonic (upper) and supersonic (lower) regions.

Rayleigh Flow Property Ratios (relative to * at M=1)
T/T* = M² × [2(γ+1)/(1+γM²)]² = 4M²(γ+1)² / (1+γM²)² for general notation p/p* = (γ+1) / (1+γM²) p₀/p₀* = [(γ+1)/(1+γM²)] × [(2+(γ-1)M²)/(γ+1)]^(γ/(γ-1)) T₀/T₀* = 2(γ+1)M² × [2+(γ-1)M²] / (1+γM²)² ρ/ρ* = (1+γM²) / [(γ+1)M²] = 1/(V/V*) For air (γ=1.4): T/T* = 2.4M²(2.4)² / (1+1.4M²)² = 2.4M² × [2.4/(1+1.4M²)]² T₀/T₀* = 2.4M²(2+0.4M²) / (1+1.4M²)²
Property Trends in Rayleigh Flow
Heat ADDED (q > 0) Subsonic (M<1)
  • T₀ increases
  • M increases toward 1
  • p decreases
  • T increases (until M=1/√γ ≈ 0.845)
  • V increases
  • p₀ decreases
Heat ADDED (q > 0) Supersonic (M>1)
  • T₀ increases
  • M decreases toward 1
  • p increases
  • T increases
  • V decreases
  • p₀ decreases
Heat Transfer Relation
q = h₀₂ - h₀₁ = Cₚ(T₀₂ - T₀₁) Using T₀/T₀* ratios at M₁ and M₂: q = CₚT₀* × [(T₀₂/T₀*) - (T₀₁/T₀*)] Maximum heat addition (thermal choking): q_max = Cₚ(T₀* - T₀₁) = CₚT₀₁ × [(T₀*/T₀₁) - 1]
3.6
Explain thermal choking in Rayleigh flow. What is maximum heat transfer? Can subsonic Rayleigh flow become supersonic by continuous heating?
Module 3🔥 All Papers3 Marks
›
Thermal Choking

In Rayleigh flow, adding heat to the flow drives Mach number toward M = 1. When M = 1 is reached at the exit — the flow is thermally choked. No further heat can be added without changing upstream conditions.

Maximum heat addition (to reach M=1 from inlet Mach M₁): q_max = Cₚ T₀₁ × [(T₀*/T₀₁) - 1] where T₀*/T₀₁ = (1+γM₁²)² / [2(γ+1)M₁²(1 + (γ-1)/2 M₁²)]^(-1) For air: T₀*/T₀₁ from Rayleigh tables at inlet M₁
Can Subsonic Rayleigh Flow Become Supersonic by Continuous Heating?

No — not in a constant area duct.

  • Adding heat to subsonic flow moves it along the Rayleigh line toward M=1 (from the upper subsonic branch).
  • To cross M=1 and enter the supersonic branch, the flow would need to jump across the maximum entropy point — which would require a decrease in entropy.
  • This violates the Second Law.
  • Therefore, a subsonic Rayleigh flow cannot become supersonic by continuous heating in a constant area duct alone.
  • To achieve supersonic flow, a convergent-divergent nozzle (area change) is required first.
📝 This question was directly asked in Jul 2023: "Can subsonic Rayleigh flow become supersonic by continuous heating? Explain with a Rayleigh line." Draw the h-s or p-v diagram and show that M=1 is a barrier.
What Happens at Maximum Heat Transfer (M₂=1/√γ)?

A curious result: in subsonic Rayleigh flow, the static temperature reaches a maximum at M = 1/√γ (for air: M = 1/√1.4 ≈ 0.845), not at M = 1. Beyond this point, adding more heat actually decreases static temperature even though stagnation temperature increases (because velocity increase dominates).

Maximum static temperature in subsonic Rayleigh: at M = 1/√γ = 0.845 (air) At this point: V = a/√γ = √(RT) — not sonic speed!
3.7
Differentiate between frictional choking (Fanno) and thermal choking (Rayleigh).
Module 3🔥 Apr 20233 Marks
›
Frictional Choking (Fanno)
  • Cause: wall friction in constant area duct
  • Adiabatic flow (no heat transfer)
  • T₀ is conserved
  • p₀ decreases due to friction
  • Both subsonic and supersonic drive toward M=1
  • Limit: maximum duct length L_max
  • Parameter: 4fL*/D
  • Fanno line in h-s space
Thermal Choking (Rayleigh)
  • Cause: heat addition in constant area duct
  • Frictionless (no friction)
  • T₀ increases (heat added)
  • p₀ decreases due to heat addition
  • Both subsonic and supersonic drive toward M=1
  • Limit: maximum heat addition q_max
  • Parameter: T₀/T₀*
  • Rayleigh line in p-v or h-s space
Key Similarity
Both Fanno and Rayleigh flows drive the Mach number toward 1 regardless of whether inlet is subsonic or supersonic. In both cases, M=1 is the maximum entropy state and represents the choking condition. The difference is the mechanism: friction (Fanno) vs heat transfer (Rayleigh).
3.8
What is the Mach number that limits the increase in static enthalpy in Rayleigh flow?
Module 3🔥 Jul 2023 Q VII-a4 Marks
›
Derivation

Static enthalpy h = CₚT in Rayleigh flow. We need the Mach number at which T (and hence h) is maximum.

T/T* = 4(γ+1)²M² / (1+γM²)² For maximum T, differentiate d(T/T*)/dM = 0: d/dM [M²/(1+γM²)²] = 0 Using quotient rule: (1+γM²)² × 2M - M² × 2(1+γM²) × 2γM = 0 Divide by 2M(1+γM²): (1+γM²) - 2γM² = 0 1 + γM² - 2γM² = 0 1 - γM² = 0 ∴ M² = 1/γ M = 1/√γ For air (γ=1.4): M = 1/√1.4 = 1/1.183 = 0.845
Physical Significance
  • At M = 1/√γ ≈ 0.845 (for air): maximum static temperature (and enthalpy) in subsonic Rayleigh flow.
  • Beyond this point (toward M=1), adding heat INCREASES T₀ but DECREASES static T (kinetic energy increases faster).
  • For supersonic Rayleigh flow, static temperature always increases with heat addition (moving from M>1 toward M=1).
  • This M = 1/√γ point is NOT the choking point — choking occurs at M = 1.
At M = 1/√γ: V = a/√γ = √(RT) [not the speed of sound!] Velocity at this point equals √(p/ρ) — called the "characteristic velocity"
