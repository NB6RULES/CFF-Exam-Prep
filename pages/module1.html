Module 1🔥 All Papers3 Marks

Mach Number Definition

Mach number M is the ratio of the local flow velocity to the local speed of sound:

M = V / a where a = √(γRT) = local speed of sound Classification: M < 1 → Subsonic flow M = 1 → Sonic flow (critical) 1 < M < 5 → Supersonic flow M > 5 → Hypersonic flow M < 0.3 → Incompressible (compressibility effects <5%)
Effect of Mach Number on Compressibility
  • At M < 0.3: density variation <5% — treated as incompressible. Bernoulli equation applies accurately.
  • At M = 0.8: compressibility causes ~17% error if incompressible equations are used (standard result — see numericals).
  • At M ≥ 1: compressibility is dominant. Density changes significantly. Shock waves can form.
  • The pressure coefficient correction: Cp_compressible = Cp_incompressible / √(1-M²) — Prandtl-Glauert rule (subsonic).
Mach Cone and Mach Angle

When an object moves at supersonic speed (M > 1), it outruns the pressure waves it generates. The envelope of these waves forms a conical wavefront — the Mach cone.

Mach angle: sin μ = a/V = 1/M At M=1: μ = 90° (normal to flow — no cone forms) At M=2: μ = 30° At M→∞: μ → 0° (very narrow cone) Mach cone half-angle μ DECREASES as M INCREASES

Inside the Mach cone: zone of action — disturbances can reach here. Outside: zone of silence — unaffected by the moving source.

📝 Always draw the Mach cone: point source moving right, two diverging lines forming the cone, label μ and V, mark zone of action vs zone of silence.

Stagnation State

The stagnation state is the hypothetical state reached when the flow is brought to rest isentropically (reversibly and adiabatically). Stagnation properties represent the total energy of the flow.

Stagnation temperature: T₀ = T + V²/(2Cₚ) ← from energy equation Stagnation enthalpy: h₀ = h + V²/2 Using T₀/T = 1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²: T₀ = T × (1 + 0.2M²) for air Stagnation pressure (isentropic process): p₀/p = (T₀/T)^(γ/(γ-1)) = (1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)^(γ/(γ-1)) p₀ = p × (1 + 0.2M²)^3.5 for air Stagnation density: ρ₀/ρ = (1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)^(1/(γ-1)) ρ₀ = ρ × (1 + 0.2M²)^2.5 for air
Critical State (denoted by *)

The critical state is the state when flow velocity equals the local speed of sound — i.e., M = 1. Critical conditions are the reference state for isentropic flow analysis.

At M = 1 (critical/throat condition): T* = T₀ × 2/(γ+1) = 0.8333 T₀ for air p* = p₀ × (2/(γ+1))^(γ/(γ-1)) = 0.5283 p₀ for air ρ* = ρ₀ × (2/(γ+1))^(1/(γ-1)) = 0.6339 ρ₀ for air a* = √(γRT*) = critical speed of sound V* = a* (velocity = speed of sound at M=1)
Adiabatic Energy Equation (Steady Flow Energy Equation)
h + V²/2 = h₀ = constant (adiabatic, no work) CₚT + V²/2 = CₚT₀ = constant T₀ - T = V²/(2Cₚ) ← kinetic energy converted to thermal energy when flow is isentropically brought to rest
💡 T₀ is conserved in adiabatic flows (even across shocks). p₀ is conserved only in isentropic flows — it drops across shocks and through ducts with friction or heat transfer.
1.3
Derive the area-velocity relationship for isentropic flow. Explain accelerating and decelerating passages for different Mach numbers.
Module 1🔥 Apr 2023 & Apr 20243–4 Marks
›
Derivation

Start from the continuity equation and Euler's equation for 1D isentropic flow:

Continuity: ρAV = constant → dρ/ρ + dA/A + dV/V = 0 ...(1) Euler (momentum): V dV = -dp/ρ For isentropic: dp = a² dρ (a = speed of sound) → dρ/ρ = -V dV/a² = -M² dV/V ...(2) Substituting (2) into (1): -M²dV/V + dA/A + dV/V = 0 dA/A = (M²-1) dV/V ∴ dA/A = (M² - 1) dV/V ← Area-Velocity Relation
Physical Interpretation
Subsonic (M < 1)
  • (M²-1) < 0
  • dA and dV have OPPOSITE signs
  • Converging duct → accelerates (like a pump nozzle)
  • Diverging duct → decelerates (like a diffuser)
Supersonic (M > 1)
  • (M²-1) > 0
  • dA and dV have SAME sign
  • Diverging duct → accelerates (supersonic nozzle)
  • Converging duct → decelerates (supersonic diffuser)
Key Conclusion
To accelerate a flow from subsonic to supersonic, the duct must first converge (to reach M=1 at throat) then diverge (to continue accelerating to M>1). This is the convergent-divergent (Laval) nozzle. At M=1: dV/V is finite and dA/A = 0 — the throat (minimum area).
1.4
Derive expression for area ratio A/A* as a function of Mach number for isentropic flow of a perfect gas. What is geometric choking?
Module 1🔥 Sept 2024 Q III-a4 Marks
›
Derivation

From continuity between any section (A, M) and the critical section (A*, M=1):

ρAV = ρ*A*V* (mass conservation, choked) A/A* = (ρ*V*) / (ρV) = (ρ*/ρ) × (V*/V) = (ρ*/ρ) × (a*/V) Using isentropic relations: ρ/ρ₀ = (1 + (γ-1)/2 M²)^(-1/(γ-1)) ρ*/ρ₀ = (2/(γ+1))^(1/(γ-1)) a* = √(γRT*), V = M×a = M×√(γRT) T*/T = (1 + (γ-1)/2 M²) / (γ+1)/2 Combining: A/A* = (1/M) × [2/(γ+1) × (1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)]^((γ+1)/2(γ-1)) For air (γ = 1.4): A/A* = (1/M) × [(1 + 0.2M²)/1.2]^3
Properties of the A/A* Function
  • For every A/A* > 1, there are TWO solutions: one subsonic M and one supersonic M.
  • Minimum A/A* = 1 occurs at M = 1 (the throat).
  • The throat (minimum area) is the only location where M = 1 can occur in a smooth isentropic flow.
Geometric Choking

Geometric choking occurs when the throat area A* reaches its minimum physical value — i.e., the throat area limits the mass flow rate. Once the throat is sonic (M=1), no further increase in mass flow is possible by reducing back pressure alone — the nozzle is choked.

Maximum mass flow (choked): ṁ_max = ρ*A*V* = p₀A*√(γ/RT₀) × (2/(γ+1))^((γ+1)/2(γ-1)) For air: ṁ_max = 0.6847 × p₀A*/√T₀ [SI units]

Steady Flow Energy Equation (SFEE)
h + V²/2 = h₀ = CₚT₀ (adiabatic, no work) Using h = CᵥT, a² = γRT = γ(γ-1)CᵥT: a²/(γ-1) + V²/2 = a₀²/(γ-1) = constant Rearranging: (a/a₀)² + (γ-1)/2 × (V/a₀)² = 1
The Adiabatic Flow Ellipse

This equation is the equation of an ellipse in (V, a) space — the adiabatic flow ellipse (also called the Fliegner ellipse). Every point on the ellipse represents a possible flow state with the same stagnation enthalpy.

  • Point A (V=0, a=a₀): Stagnation state — all energy as thermal, zero kinetic.
  • Point B (V=a*): Sonic state — M=1 critical condition. Located where 45° line from origin intersects ellipse.
  • Region V < a* (subsonic): Between A and B on upper ellipse. M < 1.
  • Region a* < V < V_max (supersonic): Between B and C. M > 1.
  • Point C (a=0, V=V_max): Maximum velocity — all energy converted to kinetic, T=0 (absolute zero). V_max = √(2CₚT₀) = a₀√(2/(γ-1)).
V_max = √(2CₚT₀) = a₀ × √(2/(γ-1)) For air: V_max = a₀ × √(2/0.4) = a₀ × √5 = 2.236 a₀ a* = a₀ × √(2/(γ+1)) = a₀ × √(2/2.4) = 0.9129 a₀ for air
📝 Draw the ellipse: horizontal axis = V/a₀, vertical axis = a/a₀. Mark point A (stagnation), B (sonic M=1), C (maximum velocity). Shade subsonic and supersonic regions.

Explain Impulse function and derive an expression for it. What is its significance?
Module 1🔥 Sept 2024 Q I-b3 Marks

Definition

The Impulse function F (also called the Thrust function) is the sum of pressure force and momentum flux at a cross-section of a duct. It is conserved in isentropic flow without external forces.

F = pA + ṁV = pA + ρAV² Dividing by p₀A*: F/(p₀A*) = (p/p₀)(A/A*)(1 + γM²) Using isentropic relations, this simplifies to a function of M only: F/(p₀A*) = (A/A*) × (p/p₀) × (1 + γM²) = f(M) — the impulse function ratio
Significance
  • Across a normal shock: impulse function F is conserved (same upstream and downstream) since p₁A₁ + ρ₁A₁V₁² = p₂A₂ + ρ₂A₂V₂².
  • Used to calculate thrust of rocket nozzles and jet engines.
  • Change in impulse function = net thrust force.
  • In nozzle design: F/F* tables available similar to A/A* tables.
Change in thrust across a section: ΔF = F₂ - F₁ = net thrust For choked nozzle: F_exit = p_exit × A_exit × (1 + γM_exit²) + (p_exit - p_amb)A_exit

C-D Nozzle Operating Regimes
  1. p_b = p₀: No flow.
  2. p_b slightly < p₀: Subsonic flow throughout, throat unchoked. Isentropic.
  3. p_b = p_c1 (first critical): Throat just reaches M=1. Entire nozzle isentropic. Diverging section acts as subsonic diffuser (flow decelerates after throat).
  4. p_c1 > p_b > p_c2: Normal shock exists in diverging section. Upstream of shock: isentropic supersonic. Shock moves toward exit as p_b decreases.
  5. p_b = p_c2: Normal shock sits exactly at exit.
  6. p_c2 > p_b > p_design: Oblique shocks form outside the nozzle (over-expanded: p_e < p_b).
  7. p_b = p_design: Fully isentropic supersonic flow to exit. Design condition.
  8. p_b < p_design: Under-expanded: expansion waves form outside exit (p_e > p_b).
Isentropic Flow Through Diffusers

A diffuser converts kinetic energy to pressure energy. For isentropic operation:

  • Subsonic diffuser: Diverging duct — velocity decreases, pressure increases. Prone to flow separation if divergence angle >~7°.
  • Supersonic diffuser: Converging duct — counterintuitive! Velocity decreases (from supersonic), pressure increases. Then must pass through M=1 and further diffuse subsonically.
  • Ideal supersonic diffuser has a converging section (to M=1) followed by a diverging section — essentially a C-D nozzle in reverse.
  • A normal shock in the diffuser throat gives minimum practical loss (diffuser design in wind tunnels).

General Mass Flow Rate
ṁ = ρAV = ρ₀(ρ/ρ₀) × A × M × a Using isentropic relations: ρ/ρ₀ = (1 + (γ-1)/2 M²)^(-1/(γ-1)) a = √(γRT) = √(γRT₀) × (T/T₀)^(1/2) = a₀ × (1 + (γ-1)/2 M²)^(-1/2) Substituting: ṁ = p₀A × M × √(γ/(RT₀)) × (1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)^(-(γ+1)/2(γ-1))
Maximum (Choked) Mass Flow Rate

Maximum ṁ occurs when M = 1 at throat (A = A*):

ṁ_max = p₀A* × √(γ/RT₀) × (2/(γ+1))^((γ+1)/2(γ-1)) For air (γ=1.4, R=287): ṁ_max = 0.6847 × p₀A* / √T₀ [kg/s, Pa, m², K] This is the MAXIMUM possible mass flow — further reducing back pressure does NOT increase flow rate once throat is choked.
