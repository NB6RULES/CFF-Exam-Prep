Pressure & Velocity Measurement
4.1
Explain the working of Prandtl Pitot static tube with a neat sketch. How is it used to measure Mach number in supersonic flow? State the Rayleigh Supersonic Pitot formula.
Module 4🔥 All Papers4–9 Marks
›
Construction
  • Inner tube (pitot): Forward-facing hole at tip → flow stagnates here → measures total pressure p₀
  • Outer tube (static): Small holes on the side surface, 8–10 diameters behind tip → undisturbed static pressure p
  • Both tubes connected to manometer/pressure transducer
Subsonic Measurement (M < 1)
No shock forms. Use isentropic relation: p₀/p = (1 + (γ-1)/2 × M²)^(γ/(γ-1)) = (1 + 0.2M²)^3.5 for air Measure p₀ (pitot) and p (static), compute M: M = √[5 × ((p₀/p)^(2/7) - 1)] Then velocity: V = M × √(γRT) = M × √(γRp/ρ)
Supersonic Measurement (M > 1) — Rayleigh Pitot Formula

Critical fact: In supersonic flow, a bow (normal) shock forms ahead of the pitot tube tip. The pitot tube measures post-shock stagnation pressure p₀₂ (not freestream p₀₁). The static taps (far back) are in undisturbed freestream, measuring p₁.

Rayleigh Supersonic Pitot Formula: p₀₂/p₁ = [(γ+1)²M₁²/(4γM₁²-2(γ-1))]^(γ/(γ-1)) × (1-γ+2γM₁²)/(γ+1) For air (γ=1.4): p₀₂/p₁ = [166.9M₁⁷] / [(7M₁²-1)^2.5] This combines: normal shock (M₁→M₂, p₁→p₂) + isentropic (M₂→0, p₂→p₀₂)
📝 KEY: In supersonic flow — bow shock ahead of pitot → p₀₂ ≠ p₀₁. Always state this. April 2024 Q I-h and Apr 2023 VIII-a both tested this concept.
4.2
Explain temperature recovery factor — definition, values for laminar and turbulent flow, and significance.
Module 4🔥 Sept 2024 & Apr 20233 Marks
›
Definition

A temperature probe in high-speed flow does not measure the full stagnation temperature T₀ due to viscous dissipation and heat transfer effects. It measures the adiabatic wall temperature T_aw. The recovery factor r is:

r = (T_aw - T) / (T₀ - T) where 0 < r ≤ 1 Rearranging: T_aw = T + r(T₀ - T) Using T₀ = T(1 + (γ-1)/2 M²): T_aw = T × [1 + r(γ-1)/2 × M²]
Values
Laminar BL: r = √Pr ≈ √0.72 ≈ 0.848 Turbulent BL: r = Pr^(1/3) ≈ 0.72^0.333 ≈ 0.896 (Prandtl number for air at normal conditions: Pr ≈ 0.72) If Pr = 1: r = 1 → probe reads true T₀ (Some diatomic gases have Pr ≈ 1)
Significance
  • All temperature probes in high-speed flows read T_aw, not T₀.
  • True T₀ = T_aw + (1-r)(T₀-T) — requires recovery factor correction.
  • Critical in supersonic wind tunnel temperature measurement, aerodynamic heating analysis.
  • Aircraft skin temperature: T_skin ≈ T_aw = T × (1 + r × 0.2M²) for air.
Optical Flow Visualization
4.3
Explain Schlieren apparatus with a neat figure. Differentiate from Shadowgraph technique.
Module 4🔥 All 4 Papers3–9 Marks
›
Shadowgraph

Principle: Based on ∂²ρ/∂x² (second derivative of density). Light bends in density gradients — the shadow on screen shows where bending changes abruptly (shocks).

Signal ∝ ∂²n/∂x² ∝ ∂²ρ/∂x²

Setup (draw): Point source → condenser lens → parallel light → test section → screen. No knife edge.

Shows: Only abrupt density changes (shocks, detonations). Cannot resolve gradual gradients.

Schlieren Apparatus

Principle: Based on ∂ρ/∂x (first derivative). Uses a knife edge to block refracted rays, creating contrast proportional to density gradient.

Signal ∝ ∂n/∂x ∝ ∂ρ/∂x (MORE sensitive than shadowgraph)

Setup (draw — label all components):

  1. Point light source (bright, small — pinhole or arc lamp)
  2. Collimating lens L1 → parallel beam through test section
  3. Focusing lens L2 → brings refracted rays to focal point
  4. Knife edge at focal point of L2 — blocks some refracted rays (critical component!)
  5. Camera/screen beyond knife edge

Shows: Both shocks AND gentle gradients (boundary layers, heat plumes, mixing layers). Knife edge orientation controls which gradient direction is visible.

Comparison
Shadowgraph
  • ∂²ρ/∂x² — second derivative
  • No knife edge
  • Simple setup
  • Only abrupt changes visible
  • Cannot detect boundary layers
  • Less sensitive
Schlieren
  • ∂ρ/∂x — first derivative
  • Knife edge essential
  • More complex
  • Gradual + abrupt changes visible
  • Boundary layers, heat plumes visible
  • More sensitive
4.4
Explain the Interferometer technique for flow visualization. How does it give quantitative density data?
Module 4🔥 All 4 Papers3–9 Marks
›
Principle

Uses wave optics: when coherent light passes through a region of varying density, its optical path length changes (refractive index n ∝ density ρ). Interference with a reference beam produces fringes — fringe shift = quantitative density change.

Gladstone-Dale: n - 1 = Kρ (K = 2.26×10⁻⁴ m³/kg for air) Optical path difference: ΔL = (n-1)L = KρL Fringe shift: Δf = ΔL/λ = K(ρ-ρ_ref)L/λ Density from fringe count: ρ = ρ_ref + Δf × λ/(K×L) → QUANTITATIVE absolute density (unlike Schlieren/Shadowgraph)
Mach-Zehnder Interferometer Setup (draw)
  1. Coherent laser source
  2. Beam splitter 1 → splits into Test beam and Reference beam
  3. Test beam through wind tunnel test section (density varies)
  4. Reference beam through undisturbed air (same path length)
  5. Beam recombiner (Beam splitter 2) → both beams merge
  6. Camera → interference fringes visible
Fringe Interpretation
  • Straight parallel fringes → uniform density (undisturbed)
  • Bent fringes → density gradient
  • Fringe shift by Δf fringes → density change = Δf × λ/(KL)
  • Near shocks: abrupt fringe shift
🔑 Advantage over Schlieren: Interferometer gives ABSOLUTE density values (quantitative). Schlieren and Shadowgraph only show gradients qualitatively.
Velocity & Temperature Sensors
4.5
Explain Hot Wire Anemometer — constant current and constant temperature types. King's Law.
Module 4🔥 All Papers4–9 Marks
›
Principle

A fine electrically heated wire (~5μm dia, ~1mm long tungsten) in the flow loses heat by convection. Heat loss rate depends on velocity → measures V from electrical output.

Heat balance: I²R_w = h×A×(T_w - T_fluid) King's Law: E² = A + B×V^n (n≈0.5 laminar, 0.45 turbulent)
CCA — Constant Current Anemometer
  • Constant current I fed to wire
  • V increases → more cooling → T_w drops → R_w drops → voltage across wire drops
  • Measure voltage → infer V via King's Law calibration
  • Slow response (~1 kHz). Risk of burnout if V drops suddenly.
CTA — Constant Temperature Anemometer (preferred)
  • Wire resistance (= temperature) kept constant via Wheatstone bridge + feedback amplifier
  • V increases → cooling → bridge imbalance → amplifier increases I → temperature restored
  • Output: bridge voltage E → V via King's Law
  • Fast response (~50–100 kHz). No burnout. Suitable for turbulence measurement.
CTA Wheatstone bridge: R₁/R₂ = R_fixed/R_w (balanced) Imbalance → error voltage → amplifier → correcting current Output bridge voltage E: E² = A + B×V^n
CCA
  • Constant current
  • Slow (~1 kHz)
  • Burnout risk
  • Simple circuit
CTA (preferred)
  • Constant temperature
  • Fast (50–100 kHz)
  • No burnout
  • Feedback bridge circuit

4.6
Explain the working of a Laser Doppler Velocimeter (Velocimeter/LDV) with a neat figure.
Module 4🔥 Sept 2024 & Apr 20243–8 Marks
›
Principle — Doppler Effect
Velocity from Doppler frequency shift: V = Δf × λ / (2 sin(θ/2)) where: Δf = Doppler frequency shift λ = laser wavelength θ = angle between two intersecting beams No physical contact with flow required — fully non-intrusive.
Dual-Beam LDV Setup (draw)
  1. Single laser beam → beam splitter → two equal beams
  2. Focusing optics: both beams cross at a small measurement volume in the flow
  3. Fringe pattern (alternating light/dark bands) formed at intersection
  4. Seeding particles (~1μm oil/TiO₂) flow through measurement volume
  5. Each particle crossing a fringe → burst of scattered light
  6. Photodetector → burst frequency = Doppler frequency Δf
  7. Signal processor → compute V
Advantages
  • Non-intrusive — no probe disturbs the flow
  • No calibration needed (direct physics-based relationship)
  • Can measure very high velocities including supersonic
  • Flow direction measurable (with frequency shifting)
  • 2D/3D velocity with multiple beam pairs
4.7
Explain the working principle of a Shock Tube with a neat sketch.
Module 4🔥 Apr 20243–4 Marks
›
Purpose

A shock tube generates controlled shock waves of known strength for short durations — used for aerodynamic testing, high-temperature gas dynamics, and combustion research without a wind tunnel.

Construction & Components (draw)
  • Driver section: High pressure gas (p₄, usually He or H₂ for strong shocks)
  • Diaphragm: Thin metal/plastic membrane, separates sections
  • Driven section: Low pressure test gas (p₁, usually air or gas under study)
  • Instrumentation ports along driven section
Working Sequence
  1. Diaphragm bursts (pressure ratio p₄/p₁ exceeds burst pressure)
  2. Incident shock wave propagates INTO driven section at speed V_s
  3. Expansion (rarefaction) wave propagates BACK into driver section
  4. Contact surface separates driver gas from test gas (they don't mix)
  5. Test gas behind shock: high T and p, uniform for short duration (μs to ms)
  6. Shock reflects off end wall → even higher T, p for ignition studies
Four regions after burst: Region 1: Undisturbed test gas (ahead of shock) Region 2: Shocked test gas (uniform T₂, p₂, V₂) ← test region Region 3: Expanded driver gas Region 4: Undisturbed driver gas Shock Mach number M_s: p₂/p₁ = (7M_s²-1)/6 (normal shock, air)
Wind Tunnels
4.8
Describe subsonic and supersonic wind tunnels. Open vs closed circuit. Special problems of supersonic tunnels.
Module 4🔥 All 4 Papers8–12 Marks
›
Subsonic Wind Tunnel (M < 1)
  • Settling chamber: Large, low-velocity. Screens and honeycombs remove turbulence.
  • Contraction (nozzle): Converging section, contraction ratio 6–10:1. Accelerates and uniformizes flow.
  • Test section: Uniform parallel subsonic flow. Model mounted here.
  • Diffuser: Expanding section — decelerates flow, recovers pressure energy. Reduces fan power.
  • Fan/drive: Blower type (fan pushes) or suction type (fan pulls).
Supersonic Wind Tunnel (M > 1)
  • High pressure reservoir / compressor: Creates high stagnation pressure upstream.
  • C-D Nozzle: Throat → M=1, diverging section → supersonic M in test section.
  • Test section: Supersonic uniform flow. Model tested.
  • Diffuser (second C-D): Decelerates supersonic flow. Normal shock at diffuser throat (minimum loss).
  • Vacuum tank or exhaust: Receives decelerated flow.
Power required scales with pressure ratio: At M=2: p₀/p_exit ≈ 7.8:1 At M=3: p₀/p_exit ≈ 36:1 → enormous power!
Open vs Closed Circuit
Open Circuit (Eiffel Type)
  • Air from atmosphere, exhausted back
  • Simple, cheap
  • External disturbances enter
  • Cannot control humidity/T
  • Higher running cost
Closed Circuit (Göttingen Type)
  • Air recirculates in closed loop
  • Energy-efficient
  • Controlled conditions
  • Can pressurize for Re control
  • Needs cooler; expensive
Special Problems of Supersonic Wind Tunnels
  1. Starting problem: During start-up, a strong starting shock travels through the test section. Diffuser throat must be large enough to swallow it.
  2. Enormous power: Pressure ratios of 10–150:1 require huge compressors or vacuum tanks. Often intermittent (blowdown) to reduce cost.
  3. Diffuser sizing: Diffuser throat must exceed nozzle throat to start; later narrowed for efficiency.
  4. Flow uniformity: Boundary layers on nozzle walls disturb core flow — must use displacement correction contours.
  5. Condensation: Moisture in air condenses during rapid expansion — must use dry air.
  6. Short run times: Intermittent tunnels run seconds — instruments must respond fast.
  7. Model forces: High dynamic pressure — stiff support stings required.
4.9
Why is it desirable to locate the shock at the diffuser throat during operation of a supersonic wind tunnel? Why must diffuser throat be larger than nozzle throat?
Module 4🔥 Sept 2024 Q IX-a4 Marks
›
Why Shock at Diffuser Throat is Desirable
  1. Minimum shock strength → minimum loss: As flow decelerates in the converging diffuser, M approaches 1 at the throat. A shock at M just above 1 is the weakest possible shock — stagnation pressure loss is nearly zero.
  2. Post-shock flow immediately subsonic: After the weak throat shock (M₂≈1), the diverging diffuser efficiently decelerates subsonic flow to low velocity.
  3. Contrast with other locations: Shock in test section (M = 2, 3, or more) would cause massive stagnation pressure loss — 28% at M=2, 67% at M=3.
Stagnation pressure loss: Shock at M=1.05: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.9997 (0.03% loss) Shock at M=2.0: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.721 (28% loss) Shock at M=3.0: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.328 (67% loss!) ∴ Minimum-loss operation → shock at diffuser throat
Why Diffuser Throat > Nozzle Throat

During start-up, a starting shock (strong, near-normal) must traverse the entire tunnel length and be swallowed by the diffuser. The diffuser throat must be large enough to pass this starting shock. If diffuser throat = nozzle throat, the starting shock cannot pass — the tunnel will not start.

Required: A*_diffuser > A*_nozzle for starting After starting (in steady supersonic operation with shock at diffuser throat): The effective stagnation pressure has dropped across the shock, so the effective throat area needed is A*_nozzle × (p₀₁/p₀₂) — which is < A*_nozzle. ∴ Diffuser throat is oversized during steady operation → some efficiency loss. Variable-geometry diffusers reduce throat after starting for better efficiency.
