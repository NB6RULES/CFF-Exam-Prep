§11.1 — Pressure Measurement
4.1
Static pressure, stagnation pressure, Prandtl Pitot-static tube, and types of pressure measuring devices.

Why Pressure is the Key Measurement

Static and stagnation pressures give the most important flow information. Density is determined from ρ = p/RT. Velocity is determined from p₀ – p. Stagnation pressure with stagnation temperature fixes the maximum mass flow rate. Difference of stagnation pressure at two points = measure of flow losses.

ṁ_max √T₀ / (A* p₀) = 0.0404 (γ=1.4, R=287 J/kg·K)
Fig 11.1 — Static pressure tappings (types a, b, c) & Fig 11.2 — (a) Static pressure probe with static holes, (b) Pitot tube measuring stagnation pressure p₀
§11.1.1 Static Pressure

Static pressure is the pressure transmitted by an orifice in the plane of the flow to a measuring device — called a static pressure hole. It must be small, drilled neatly, away from flow disturbances, free from burrs. Diameter: 0.25 mm to 2.5 mm. When measuring away from a wall, a static pressure tube (Fig 11.2a) is used — leading end closed, static holes on surface, accurate to ±5° yaw.

§11.1.2 Stagnation Pressure — Pitot Tube

The pitot tube (Fig 11.2b) — a bent stainless steel tube with open end facing the flow — decelerates the flow nearly reversibly to stagnation conditions. Recorded pressure ≈ stagnation pressure p₀. Accurate to ±20° from flow direction. p₀ is a measure of entropy level — its change indicates entropy rise due to irreversibility.

§11.1.3 Mass Flow Weighted Mean Pressures
Mean static pressure over circular duct (Eq. 11.4): p̄ = ∫₀ᴿ 2πρcpr dr / ∫₀ᴿ 2πρcr dr Mean stagnation pressure (if ρ,p nearly constant, Eq. 11.7): p̄₀ = p + ½ρc²̄ (standard form)

Bourdon tube: Most widely used mechanical gauge. Non-circular cross-section tube tends toward circular under pressure — free end moves, magnified for reading. C-type or spiral type.

Electrical transducers: Based on resistance, capacitance, inductance, thermal effects, or piezoelectric effect. Essential for fluctuating/unsteady pressures.

Pirani (thermal) gauge: Thin wire in Wheatstone bridge; heated to ~250°C. At low pressures fewer gas molecules → slower cooling → less current to maintain temperature → current = pressure measure.

Prandtl Pitot-Static Tube — Supersonic (Rayleigh Pitot Formula)

In supersonic flow (M > 1), a bow (normal) shock forms ahead of the pitot tube. The tube measures post-shock stagnation pressure p₀₂, not freestream p₀₁. Static taps (far back) measure freestream p₁.

Rayleigh Supersonic Pitot Formula: p₀₂/p₁ = [(γ+1)²M₁²/(4γM₁²-2(γ-1))]^(γ/(γ-1)) × (1-γ+2γM₁²)/(γ+1) For air (γ=1.4): p₀₂/p₁ = [166.9 M₁⁷] / [(7M₁²-1)^2.5] Subsonic (M<1): no shock → isentropic: p₀/p = (1+0.2M²)^3.5
📝 Always state: In supersonic flow a bow shock forms ahead of pitot tube → p₀₂ (post-shock) ≠ p₀₁ (freestream). This is directly tested in Apr 2024 Q I-h and Apr 2023 VIII-a.

Why Probes Don't Read True T or T₀

The temperature Tᵣ recorded by any probe is neither static T nor stagnation T₀. Four causes (§11.2):

  • (a) Shocks may develop near the probe at high velocity — interferes with measurement
  • (b) Boundary layers surround probe; viscous dissipation raises temperature beyond what deceleration alone would give
  • (c) If probe is hotter than surroundings — heat flows from probe to gas
  • (d) Outward heat conduction through thermocouple wires — makes process non-adiabatic
§11.2.1 Correction Factor & §11.2.2 Recovery Factor
Correction factor (Eq. 11.8): Kₜ = (Tᵣ - T) / (T₀ - T) Recovery factor (adiabatic conditions — cause (d) eliminated): Rƒ = (T_aw - T) / (T₀ - T) ...(11.9) T_aw/T = 1 + Rƒ × (γ-1)/2 × M² ...(11.11) Textbook values (§11.2.2): Subsonic flow: Rƒ = 0.89 Supersonic flow: Rƒ = 0.86 Theoretical (Prandtl number based, air Pr=0.72): Laminar BL: Rƒ = √Pr ≈ 0.848 Turbulent BL: Rƒ = Pr^(1/3) ≈ 0.896
Fig 11.5 — Comparison of various temperatures vs Mach number: T₀ (stagnation), T_aw (adiabatic wall), Tᵣ (recorded), T (static). All diverge at higher M.
§11.2.5 Resistance Thermometers & §11.2.6 Thermocouples
Resistance thermometer (Eq. 11.12): Rₜ = R₀(1 + C₁T + C₂T² + ··· + CₙTⁿ) Metals: platinum (−270°C to 1000°C), nickel, copper. Wheatstone bridge circuit: R₃ = sensing element, R₄ = variable for null point (Fig 11.6). Thermocouple EMF (Eq. 11.13): Vₐ - V_b = ∫[T₀ to T] (S*_A - S*_B) dT Key pairs (Table 11.2): Copper-constantan: −200 to 350°C, 60 µV/°C Iron-constantan: −200 to 1300°C Chromel-alumel: 0 to 1500°C Rhodium-iridium: up to 2000°C
Fig 11.7 — Thermocouple circuit (ice bath reference + high temperature junction) & Fig 11.8 — Stagnation temperature probe: thermocouple + vents + shield + heat insulator → Rƒ ≈ 1
§11.2.7 Stagnation Temperature Probe (High Velocity Total Temperature Probe)

Thermojunction inserted into a total pressure tube (Figure 11.8). Key features:

  • Shield: Minimises heat losses due to conduction and radiation
  • Heat insulators: Enclose thermocouple wires — further reduce conduction losses
  • Vents: Allow fresh gas sample around thermojunction
  • Result: recovery factor Rƒ ≈ 1 — near-true stagnation temperature measurement

§11.4.4 Vane Anemometer

Tiny windmill with flat plate blades (generally 8), mounted on freely rotating shaft. RPM of shaft = measure of air velocity — transmitted to pointer via worm-and-wormwheel reduction gear. Good for low velocities in small pipes and ducts. Calibrated by standard pitot-static tube.

Hot-Wire Anemometer — CTA (§11.5.1) and CCA (§11.5.2). Circuit diagrams (Fig 11.13, 11.15), King's Law, calibration.
Module 4🔥 All Papers4–12 Marks

§11.5 Principle

Tiny wire (d ≈ 0.005 mm) heated electrically. When placed in gas flow, it cools due to convection. Rate of cooling depends on velocity. Two modes:

(a) Maintain constant temperature T (constant Rᵥ = R₄) → current varies → CONSTANT TEMPERATURE (CTA) (b) Maintain constant current I → resistance varies with velocity → CONSTANT CURRENT (CCA) King's Law (calibration): E² = A + B × c^n (n ≈ 0.5) Wire material: tungsten. Max temperature: ~700°C.
§11.5.1 Constant Temperature Hot-Wire (CTA) — Fig 11.13

Wire element (Rᵥ = R₄) held between two needle supports. l/d ratio ≈ 200 — reduces heat conduction from wire to supports to negligible value. Wheatstone bridge: R₁, R₄(=Rᵥ), R₂, R₃ (fixed) and variable R₅. Initial balance: R₁ adjusted. When velocity changes → cooling → R₄ changes → bridge imbalanced → R₅ readjusted to rebalance → different current for each velocity. Output to oscilloscope.

Calibration (Fig 11.14): two curves at two wire temperatures. Higher T_wire → higher sensitivity. Used for turbulence and unsteady flow — small element, negligible disturbance to flow.

Fig 11.13 — Bridge circuit for a constant temperature hot-wire anemometer. R₁, R₂, R₃, R₄(=Rᵥ=hot wire), R₅(variable). Output to oscilloscope.
§11.5.2 Constant Current Hot-Wire (CCA) — Fig 11.15

Current kept constant. Temperature (hence resistance) varies with velocity. A micro/milli voltmeter records voltage variation across the wire with gas velocity. Calibration curve shown in Fig 11.16. Limited frequency response — NOT as versatile as CTA. Performance in fluctuating flows improved by dynamic compensation.

Fig 11.14 — Calibration curves (current vs fluid velocity) for CTA at higher and lower wire temperatures. Fig 11.15 — Constant current hot-wire circuit: constant current source → hot wire → voltmeter V + amplifier → output.
CCA — Constant Current (§11.5.2)
  • Current = constant
  • Temperature/resistance varies
  • Output: voltage drop across wire
  • Limited frequency response
  • Improved by dynamic compensation
CTA — Constant Temperature (§11.5.1)
  • Temperature/resistance = constant (Rᵥ = R₄)
  • Current varies via R₅ + Wheatstone bridge
  • Output: bridge current (different per velocity)
  • High frequency response (~50 kHz)
  • Best for turbulence and unsteady flows

§11.11 — Introduction

Laser = Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. Scattered light from minute solid particles (~1 micron) in the flow is used as the velocity signal. Two systems:

  • (a) Laser Doppler Velocimeter (LDV/LDA) — Doppler frequency shift
  • (b) Dual-Focus Laser Velocimeter — flight time between two focal volumes

Lasers: He-Ne (0.5–100 mW, low cost, high reliability), Argon-ion (5–15 W), CO₂ (1–100 W). Higher power → greater noise. Seeding particles (~1 µm) artificially introduced or natural particles in flow.

§11.11.1 LDV — Dual Beam, Forward Scatter (Fig 11.40)
  1. Laser → beam splitter → two equal-intensity beams
  2. Transmitting optics (splitter, lenses) → lens A focuses both beams at measuring/probe volume M [<1 mm³] in flow
  3. Fringe pattern (alternating light/dark bands) at probe volume
  4. Seeding particles pass through → each fringe crossing produces a scattered light pulse
  5. Collecting lens B → photodetector → signal processor → Doppler frequency Δf → velocity
V = Δf × λ / (2 sin(θ/2)) Δf = Doppler shift, λ = wavelength, θ = angle between beams
Fig 11.40 — Dual-beam laser Doppler velocimeter (forward scatter): laser → beam splitter → lens A → probe volume M → collecting lens B → photodetector → signal processor → output.
LDV — Backscatter Mode (Fig 11.41)

The entire laser system is located on one side of the test section — makes traversing easier. Light scattered from probe volume M is collected back via the same lens A → mirror → lens B → photodetector. Only one transparent window needed. Requires comparatively more laser power than forward scatter.

Fig 11.41 — LDV backscatter mode: entire system on one side, mirror at 45° collects scattered light. Fig 11.42 — Dual-focus laser velocimeter (forward scatter): two focal volumes A and B separated by distance x; transit time c = x/t.
§11.11.2 Dual-Focus Laser Velocimeter (Fig 11.42 & 11.43)
Two cylindrical lenses + beam splitter → two focal volumes A and B, separation = x (x ≈ 20× beam waist width, each waist ~10 µm) Particle crosses A → START signal to clock Same particle crosses B → STOP signal → transit time t Velocity: c = x / t Backscatter version (Fig 11.43): optics on one side, two transparent windows needed on one wall. Advantage: single-sided access to flow field.
§11.11.3 Advantages of Laser Techniques
  • Non-intrusive — does not disturb the flow at all
  • Measures velocities in regions inaccessible to other devices — ideal for boundary layers
  • No calibration required — independent of pressure, temperature, and density
  • Wide range: 0.1–300 m/s; very high frequency response
  • Velocity varies linearly with signal over wide range
  • Easily interfaced with a computer
  • Applications: turbomachinery, wind tunnels, water tunnels, combustion, heat exchangers, aerospace, nuclear engineering

§11.10.1 Shadow Technique (Shadowgraph) — Fig 11.37

A collimating lens provides a parallel beam of light from a source through the transparent walls of the test section. Shadow picture obtained directly on a white screen on the other side — no knife edge, no focusing lens required.

Principle: Degree of brightness ∝ ∂²ρ/∂x² (second derivative of density) Advantages: inexpensive equipment, easy to operate. Best for: flows with SHOCKS and COMBUSTION (abrupt density changes).
Fig 11.37 — Shadowgraph: Light source → collimating lens → test section (x,y,z) → screen. No knife edge. Fig 11.38 — Mach-Zehnder interferometer: splitters A & C, mirrors B & D, M₁, M₂, test section, reference section, screen.
§11.10.2 Interferometer — Mach-Zehnder (Fig 11.38)

Variation of density is directly determined from the interference pattern on screen. Most widely used apparatus: Mach-Zehnder interferometer (Fig 11.38).

Components: Two plane half-silvered mirrors (splitters A and C), two fully reflecting mirrors B and D, light source through lens L and concave mirror M₁.

Working: Splitter A splits beam into test beam (→ test section → mirror D → splitter C → M₂) and reference beam (→ mirror B → reference section with identical compensating walls → splitter C → M₂). Both beams recombine at M₂ → fringe pattern on screen.

No flow → uniform fringes (beams in phase) With flow → density variation → optical path difference → fringe shift Gladstone-Dale: n - 1 = Kρ, K = 2.26×10⁻⁴ m³/kg (air) Density: ρ = ρ_ref + (Δf × λ) / (K × L) → QUANTITATIVE absolute density values. Expensive, difficult to operate.
§11.10.3 Schlieren Technique — Fig 11.39

Density gradient ∂ρ/∂x is obtained from varying brightness on screen. Degree of brightness is proportional to density gradient.

Setup (Fig 11.39): Light source → concave mirror M₁ (collimated beam) → test section → concave mirrors M₂ and M₃ → screen. A sharp knife edge at the focal point of M₂ intercepts about half the light. Without flow: uniform illumination. With flow: refracted rays → more/less light escapes knife edge → varying intensity.

Principle: Degree of brightness ∝ ∂ρ/∂x (first derivative — more sensitive than shadowgraph) Knife edge orientation controls which gradient direction is visible. Shows: shocks + boundary layers + heat plumes + mixing zones.
Fig 11.38 — Mach-Zehnder interferometer (detail): splitters A & C, mirrors B & D, test section, reference section, M₂, screen. Fig 11.39 — Schlieren arrangement: light source, M₁, test section, knife edge at focal point of M₂, M₃, screen.

§11.8.6 — Shock Tube

Construction and working of a Shock Tube with neat sketch (Fig 11.34). Advantages and limitations.
Module 4🔥 Apr 20243–4 Marks

Definition

A shock tube is a blowdown or intermittent tunnel in which high Mach number flow is generated by passing a shock wave through a stagnant gas in a duct.

Fig 11.34 — Shock tube: HP section (left) | Diaphragm | LP section with Model in test section | Moving shock wave (right). Shock propagates into LP section after diaphragm ruptures.
Construction & Working
  • HP section: High pressure driver gas (He or H₂ for strong shocks)
  • Diaphragm: Metal/plastic membrane separating sections
  • LP section: High vacuum; contains test gas and model
  1. Pressure in HP section increased until diaphragm ruptures
  2. Strong shock wave propagates into LP section — imparts high velocity to test gas
  3. Expansion wave propagates back into HP section
  4. Contact surface separates driver and test gases
  5. Test gas behind shock: high T and p, uniform conditions for millisecond duration
Four regions after burst: Region 4: Undisturbed HP gas Region 3: HP gas processed by expansion wave Region 2: Test gas behind shock ← uniform T₂, p₂ [TEST REGION] Region 1: Undisturbed LP test gas (ahead of shock) p₂/p₁ = (7M_s²-1)/6 [normal shock, air]
Limitation
  • High vacuum in LP side → ambient air leaks through joints/porous walls
  • Serious at high Mach numbers
  • Solution: specially designed joints, non-porous materials
Great Advantage
  • Absence of LOW temperature in test section
  • High stagnation temperature representative of actual hypersonic conditions
  • Ideal for combustion and chemical kinetics studies

Subsonic and supersonic wind tunnels — open circuit, blowdown, closed circuit types. Special problems of supersonic wind tunnels. (§11.8, §11.8.5)

Subsonic Wind Tunnels (M < 1)
  • Settling chamber: Screens + honeycombs remove turbulence; stagnation conditions p₀₁, T₀₁ established
  • Contraction nozzle: Converging section (ratio 6–10:1), accelerates and uniformizes flow
  • Test section: Uniform subsonic flow; model mounted here
  • Diffuser: Diverging section — decelerates, recovers pressure energy, reduces drive power
  • Fan/drive: Blower type (pushes) or suction type (pulls)
§11.8.5 Supersonic Wind Tunnels — Three Types
Fig 11.32 — Open circuit continuous type supersonic wind tunnel: Reservoir (p₀₁, T₀₁) → quick opening valve → throat → nozzle → test section (M>1, A, ρ, T, c, a, M) → model. Powered by compressor.
Open Circuit Continuous Type (Fig 11.32)

Compressor continuously supplies reservoir. High pressure air expanded from stagnation state (p₀₁, T₀₁) through C-D nozzle to test section. M = f(p/p₀₁) (Eq 11.37). All air must pass through drier. Compressor power very high — kinetic energy wasted to atmosphere. Cross-sectional area of test section determined by compressor capacity (Eq 11.39).

Blowdown (Intermittent) Type

High pressure air stored in large reservoirs. Quick opening valve between reservoir and nozzle starts tunnel. Operation: 1/4 to 5 minutes. Only power needed is to refill reservoirs — economical. A constant pressure valve maintains entry conditions. Can also use high vacuum downstream (atmospheric air as supply).

Fig 11.33(a) — Closed circuit continuous type supersonic wind tunnel: Reservoir → nozzle → test section (M>1) → diffuser → receiver (p₀₂, T₀₂) → cooler → drier → compressor → back to reservoir. Starting shock visible. A*_nozzle = A₁, A*_diffuser = A₂ = A_d.
Closed Circuit Continuous Type (Fig 11.33a)

Compressor + reservoir + nozzle + test section + supersonic diffuser + downstream receiver + drier + cooler. The high KE at test section exit is partly recovered by the supersonic diffuser → compressor receives gas at higher pressure → reduced compressor pressure ratio and power (p_r = p₀₁/p₀₂). Any gas besides air can be used. T₀₁ continuously rises → cooler required before compressor (cooling before compression is more efficient than after).

Compressor pressure ratio: p_r = p₀₁/p₀₂ (losses only from shock in diffuser) Cooling before compression: W_c = ṁCₚTᵢ(p_r^((γ-1)/γ) - 1) Since T_i(cooled) < T_i(uncooled): W_c < W_c' (less work needed)
Open Circuit
  • All air from atmosphere
  • All air passes through drier
  • Only air can be used
  • KE wasted to atmosphere
  • Lower capital cost
Closed Circuit
  • Gas recirculates in closed loop
  • Small drier (not all gas passes)
  • Any gas can be used
  • KE partly recovered by diffuser
  • Needs cooler; higher capital cost
Special Problems of Supersonic Wind Tunnels
  1. Starting problem: Starting shock must traverse entire test section — diffuser throat must be large enough to swallow it. Model experiences transient high loads.
  2. High power: M=2 → p₀/p ≈ 7.8:1; M=3 → 36:1. Huge compressors or vacuum tanks required.
  3. Diffuser throat sizing: A*_diffuser > A*_nozzle required for starting. Reduced after starting in variable-geometry designs.
  4. T₀ rise (closed circuit): Continuous energy dissipation raises T₀ → cooler mandatory.
  5. Condensation: Rapid nozzle expansion → moisture condensation → thoroughly dried air essential.
  6. Short run time (blowdown): 1/4 to 5 min — all instruments must respond fast.
  7. Model mounting: Very high dynamic pressure → rigid sting supports required.
4.9
Why locate shock at diffuser throat? Why must diffuser throat be larger than nozzle throat?
Module 4🔥 Sept 2024 Q IX-a4 Marks
›
Why Shock at Diffuser Throat
  1. Weakest possible shock → minimum stagnation pressure loss (p₀ loss ∝ shock strength). At diffuser throat M → 1; shock at near-M=1 is extremely weak → p₀₂/p₀₁ → 1.
  2. Minimum compressor pressure ratio in closed-circuit tunnel: p_r = p₀₁/p₀₂. Minimum loss → minimum compressor work.
  3. Post-shock flow immediately subsonic: After weak shock at throat (M₂ ≈ 1), diverging diffuser efficiently decelerates subsonically.
M_shock=1.05: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.9997 (0.03% loss) M_shock=2.0: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.721 (28% loss) M_shock=3.0: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.328 (67% loss) → Shock near M=1 at diffuser throat → minimum power
Why A*_diffuser > A*_nozzle
During start-up: starting shock (strong, near-normal) must be swallowed by diffuser. → Diffuser throat must be large enough to accommodate this starting shock. After starting (steady state): Stagnation pressure dropped → p₀₂ < p₀₁ Effective A* needed = A*_nozzle × (p₀₁/p₀₂) < A*_nozzle → Diffuser throat is oversized in steady state. Variable-geometry diffuser: throat narrowed after starting for efficiency. Fixed-geometry diffuser: accepts the steady-state inefficiency.
Write This Conclusion
Shock at diffuser throat is desirable because it is the weakest possible shock (minimum stagnation pressure loss, minimum compressor work). The diffuser throat must be larger than the nozzle throat to allow the strong starting shock to be swallowed during tunnel start-up. After starting, variable-geometry diffusers reduce the throat toward ideal for efficient steady-state operation.
