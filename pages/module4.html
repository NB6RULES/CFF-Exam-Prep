Why Pressure is the Key Measurement

Static and stagnation pressures are the most important measurable quantities in compressible flow. Density is determined from ρ = p/RT. Velocity is determined from p₀ – p. Stagnation pressure combined with stagnation temperature fixes the maximum mass flow rate through a nozzle. Difference of stagnation pressure at two points gives a measure of losses in the flow.

ṁ_max √T₀ / (A* p₀) = 0.0404 (γ=1.4, R=287 J/kg·K) [from §11.1]
§11.1.1 Static Pressure

Static pressure of a fluid stream over a surface is the pressure transmitted by an orifice in the plane of the flow to a measuring device. This orifice is a static pressure hole. Requirements:

  • Hole must be small, drilled neatly, away from flow disturbances
  • Free from burrs and other imperfections
  • Hole diameter: 0.25 mm to 2.5 mm (larger → streamline disturbance error; smaller → slow response time)

When static pressure must be measured away from a wall, a static pressure probe (Fig 11.2a) is used — leading end closed, static holes drilled on surface at a suitable section. Aligned parallel to flow (accurate to ±5°).

§11.1.2 Stagnation Pressure — Pitot Tube

Since stagnation pressure p₀ is a measure of the entropy level of the fluid, its change indicates entropy increase due to irreversibility. The pitot tube (Fig 11.2b) — a bent stainless steel tube inserted into the flow with its open end facing the flow — brings the flow to near-rest conditions. This deceleration is nearly reversible → recorded pressure ≈ stagnation pressure p₀. Accurate to ±20° from flow direction.

If a fluid is accelerated from an infinite reservoir reversibly: p_reservoir = p₀_jet. Similarly in a reversible nozzle, stagnation pressure throughout equals ambient pressure.

§11.1.3 Mass Flow Weighted Mean Pressures
Mean static pressure over circular duct cross-section: p̄ = ∫₀ᴿ p dṁ / ∫₀ᴿ dṁ = ∫₀ᴿ 2πρcpr dr / ∫₀ᴿ 2πρcr dr ...(11.4) If ρ and p don't vary radially → reduces to p (1D case). Prandtl Pitot-Static Tube — Supersonic Flow

In supersonic flow (M > 1), a bow (normal) shock forms ahead of the pitot tube tip. The tube therefore measures post-shock stagnation pressure p₀₂ (not freestream p₀₁). The static taps (far back on the outer tube) measure freestream static pressure p₁.

Rayleigh Supersonic Pitot Formula: p₀₂/p₁ = [(γ+1)²M₁²/(4γM₁²-2(γ-1))]^(γ/(γ-1)) × (1-γ+2γM₁²)/(γ+1) For air (γ=1.4): p₀₂/p₁ = [166.9 M₁⁷] / [(7M₁²-1)^2.5] Subsonic (M<1): no shock → isentropic: p₀/p = (1+0.2M²)^3.5
📝 Key distinction always to write: In supersonic flow a bow shock forms ahead of pitot tube → tube reads p₀₂ (post-shock), NOT p₀₁ (freestream). Use Rayleigh formula for M>1. Use isentropic formula for M<1. This distinction is tested in Apr 2024 Q I-h and Apr 2023 VIII-a.

Why Probes Don't Read True T or T₀

At high fluid velocities, the temperature Tᵣ recorded by any thermometer or temperature probe is neither the static temperature T nor the stagnation temperature T₀. The difference is appreciable and cannot be neglected for M > 0.3. Causes (from §11.2):

  • (a) At high velocity, shocks may develop near the probe — interferes with correct measurement
  • (b) Boundary layers surround and separate the probe from free stream; energy dissipated in BL raises temperature near probe (beyond rise due to deceleration)
  • (c) If probe is hotter than surroundings — heat flows from probe to gas
  • (d) Outward heat conduction through stem or thermocouple connecting wires — makes process non-adiabatic
§11.2.1 Correction Factor Kₜ

To account for all above factors, a correction factor Kₜ is defined (Eq. 11.8):

Kₜ = (Tᵣ - T) / (T₀ - T) ...(11.8) where: Tᵣ = temperature recorded by probe T = true static temperature T₀ = true stagnation temperature
§11.2.2 Recovery Factor Rƒ

If temperature is measured under adiabatic conditions (no outward heat transfer — condition (d) eliminated), the measured value is the adiabatic wall temperature T_aw. The recovery factor Rƒ is:

Rƒ = (T_aw - T) / (T₀ - T) ...(11.9) Combining with T₀ = T + c²/(2Cₚ) and Rƒ = 2Cₚ(T_aw-T)/c² ...(11.10): T_aw/T = 1 + Rƒ × (γ-1)/2 × M² ...(11.11) Standard values used in practice (§11.2.2 textbook): Subsonic flow: Rƒ = 0.89 Supersonic flow: Rƒ = 0.86 Theoretical values (Prandtl number based): Laminar BL: Rƒ = √Pr ≈ 0.848 (air, Pr≈0.72) Turbulent BL: Rƒ = Pr^(1/3) ≈ 0.896

Rƒ depends on the type of probe, growth of the boundary layer, and Mach number. Figure 11.5 in the textbook shows how T₀, T_aw, Tᵣ, and T vary with Mach number — all four curves diverge increasingly at higher M.

💡 Note: The textbook gives Rƒ = 0.89 (subsonic), 0.86 (supersonic) as practical values. The theoretical Prandtl-number-based values differ slightly. Cite both in your answer for full coverage.
§11.2.5 Resistance Thermometers
Rₜ = R₀(1 + C₁T + C₂T² + ··· + CₙTⁿ) ...(11.12) Metals: copper, nickel, platinum. Platinum: range −270°C to 1000°C. Flat grid pasted on surface → Wheatstone bridge circuit (Fig 11.6): Sensing element R₃ in one arm; R₄ variable for null point. Linear calibration: R vs T from separate experiment.

EMF generated between two junctions of dissimilar metals at T and T₀: Vₐ - V_b = ∫[T₀ to T] (S*_A - S*_B) dT ...(11.13) Thermocouple pairs (Table 11.2): Copper-constantan: −200 to 350°C, 60 µV/°C at 350°C Platinum-rhodium: −150 to 1000°C Iron-constantan: −200 to 1300°C Chromel-alumel: 0 to 1500°C Pt-Pt/rhodium: 6 µV/°C at 100°C Rhodium iridium-rhodium: up to 2000°C Wire diameter: 0.5 to 2.5 mm. Measured by potentiometer or milli/micro voltmeter (Fig 11.7).
§11.2.7 Stagnation Temperature Probe (High Velocity Total Temperature Probe)

To measure stagnation temperature directly, the thermojunction is inserted into a total pressure tube to create near-stagnation conditions (Figure 11.8). Key design features:

  • Shield: Minimises heat losses due to conduction and radiation
  • Heat insulators: Enclose thermocouple wires to further reduce conduction losses
  • Vents: Let in fresh gas sample around thermojunction
  • Result: recovery factor approaching unity (Rƒ ≈ 1)

§11.5 Principle

A hot-wire anemometer consists of a tiny wire (d ≈ 0.005 mm = 5 µm) held between two prongs; heated to a given temperature by electric current. Its resistance depends on temperature (Eq. 11.12). When introduced into gas flow, it cools due to convective heat transfer. Two operating modes:

(a) Wire maintained at constant temperature T → constant resistance Rᵥ = R₄ → increase current to balance cooling → CONSTANT TEMPERATURE type (b) Heating current kept constant → resistance varies with velocity → voltage changes with velocity → CONSTANT CURRENT type
§11.5.1 Constant Temperature Hot-Wire (CTA) — Fig 11.13

The wire element (Rᵥ = R₄) is held between two needle supports; l/d ratio ≈ 200 to reduce heat conduction to supports. Circuit: Wheatstone bridge with R₁, R₄(=Rᵥ), R₂, R₃ fixed and variable R₅. Initial balance: R₁ adjusted. When gas velocity changes → cooling changes Rᵥ → bridge unbalanced → R₅ readjusted to restore balance. Each velocity gives a different bridge current — this is the calibration.

Calibration curves (Figure 11.14): gas velocity vs current, at two wire temperatures. Higher wire temperature → higher sensitivity. Maximum wire temperature: ~700°C (burnout beyond this).

CTA is widely used for turbulence and unsteady flow measurement — small sensing element causes negligible disturbance.

King's Law: E² = A + B × c^n (c = velocity, n ≈ 0.5) Calibration: rotating arm moves wire at known speeds → measure current Output to oscilloscope for turbulence measurement
§11.5.2 Constant Current Hot-Wire (CCA) — Fig 11.15

The current through the hot-wire element is kept constant. The temperature (and hence resistance) changes with flow velocity. A micro or milli voltmeter across the wire records the variation of voltage with gas velocity. Calibration curve: Figure 11.16.

Circuit (Fig 11.15): Constant current source → hot wire element. Voltmeter V records voltage drop variation with velocity. Amplifier improves frequency response. Limitation: frequency response is limited — NOT as versatile as CTA. Performance in fluctuating flows improved by dynamic compensation.
CCA — Constant Current (§11.5.2)
  • Current = constant
  • Temperature/resistance varies
  • Output: voltage drop across wire
  • Limited frequency response
  • Simpler circuit; amplifier for compensation
CTA — Constant Temperature (§11.5.1)
  • Temperature/resistance = constant
  • Current varies via R₅/Wheatstone bridge
  • Output: bridge current for each velocity
  • High frequency response (~50 kHz)
  • Ideal for turbulence and unsteady flows

§11.11 Introduction

Laser = Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. Strong, monochromatic, coherent light source. The scattered light from minute solid particles in the flow (natural or artificially seeded at ~1 micron) is used as a velocity signal. Two widely used systems:

  • (a) Laser Doppler Velocimeter (LDV/LDA) — Doppler frequency shift
  • (b) Dual-Focus Laser Velocimeter — particle flight time between two focal volumes

Lasers used: Helium-Neon (0.5–100 mW, lower cost, higher reliability), Argon-ion (5–15 W), CO₂ (1–100 W). Higher power → greater noise.

§11.11.1 Laser Doppler Velocimeter (LDV) — Dual-Beam

When light is scattered by a moving particle, the scattered light has a Doppler frequency shift Δf proportional to particle velocity. Two modes:

Forward scatter (Fig 11.40): transmitting and receiving optics on opposite sides of test section. Lower laser power required.

Backscatter (Fig 11.41): entire system on one side. More convenient for traversing; needs higher laser power.

Setup (dual-beam, forward scatter, Fig 11.40):

  1. Laser beam → beam splitter → two equal beams
  2. Transmitting optics (beam splitter, lenses) → lens A focuses both beams at measuring/probe volume M [<1 mm³]
  3. Fringe pattern (alternating light/dark bands) formed at probe volume
  4. Seeding particles (~1 µm) pass through probe volume → scattered light pulses as each fringe is crossed
  5. Collecting lens B → photodetector → signal processor → Doppler frequency Δf → velocity
V = Δf × λ / (2 sin(θ/2)) where: Δf = Doppler frequency shift (measured) λ = laser wavelength θ = angle between two laser beams
§11.11.2 Dual-Focus Laser Velocimeter

Layout: Fig 11.42 (forward scatter) and Fig 11.43 (backscatter). Two cylindrical lenses + beam splitter focus beams at two focal volumes A and B, separated by distance x (≈ 20× beam waist width, microscopic ~10 µm width).

Working principle (transit time method): Particle passes through focal volume A → START signal to clock Same particle passes through focal volume B → STOP signal to clock Transit time: t = time between start and stop Fluid velocity: c = x / t Backscatter version (Fig 11.43): Scattered light from A and B collected BACK on same side as laser Only one transparent window needed on test section Entire laser + detector system on one side → easy traversing
§11.11.3 Advantages of Laser Techniques
  • Non-intrusive — does not disturb the flow (no probe inserted)
  • Measures velocities in regions inaccessible by other devices — excellent for boundary layer measurements
  • No calibration required — working is independent of pressure, temperature, and fluid density
  • Wide velocity range: 0.1 – 300 m/s; very high frequency response
  • Velocity varies linearly with signal over wide range
  • Easily interfaced with a computer
  • Applications: turbomachinery, wind tunnels, water tunnels, combustion studies, heat exchangers, aerospace, nuclear engineering

§11.10.1 Shadow Technique (Shadowgraph) — Fig 11.37

The arrangement shown in Figure 11.37 is often referred to as a shadowgraph. A collimating lens provides a collimated (parallel) beam of light from a source. This beam passes through the transparent walls of the test section. The shadow picture of the flow is directly obtained on a white screen placed on the other side of the test section.

Principle: Degree of brightness on screen ∝ ∂²ρ/∂x² (second spatial derivative of density) Setup (draw Fig 11.37): Light source → Collimating lens → Test section (transparent walls) → Screen NO knife edge · NO focusing lens Gives clear pictures of density variations in flows with SHOCKS and COMBUSTION. Equipment is INEXPENSIVE and EASY to operate — main advantage.
§11.10.2 Interferometer Technique (Mach-Zehnder) — Fig 11.38

The variation of density in the flow field is directly determined from the interference pattern on screen or photographic plate. The most widely used apparatus is the Mach-Zehnder interferometer.

Construction (Fig 11.38): Two plane half-silvered mirrors (splitters A and C) and two fully reflecting mirrors B and D. Parallel beam from light source through lens L and concave mirror M₁. Splitter A splits beam into:

  • Test beam: Part reflected by A → transparent test section → reflected by mirror D → to M₂ through splitter C
  • Reference beam: Part from A → mirror B → reference section (identical transparent walls = compensating plates) → to M₂ via splitter C
  • Both beams recombine at M₂ → screen → interference fringes
Principle: No flow → both beams in phase → uniform fringes (screen uniformly lit) With flow → density variation in test section → optical path difference → beams out of phase → interference fringe pattern on screen Gladstone-Dale: n - 1 = Kρ, K = 2.26×10⁻⁴ m³/kg (air) Fringe shift: Δf = K(ρ - ρ_ref)L/λ → ρ = ρ_ref + Δf·λ/(K·L) QUANTITATIVE density data Suitable for QUANTITATIVE density measurement. Requires EXPENSIVE equipment, difficult to operate.
§11.10.3 Schlieren Technique — Fig 11.39

The density gradient ∂ρ/∂x in the flow field is obtained from the varying degree of brightness on the screen. Degree of brightness is proportional to the density gradient.

Setup (Figure 11.39):

  1. Light source → concave mirror M₁ → collimated beam through test section
  2. Beam from test section reflected via concave mirrors M₂ and M₃ to screen
  3. Sharp knife edge inserted at focal point of mirror M₂ — intercepts about half the light
  4. Without flow: screen uniformly illuminated (half light passes knife edge)
  5. With flow: refraction in variable-density field → rays deflected → more/less light passes knife edge → varying intensity on screen
Principle: Degree of brightness ∝ ∂ρ/∂x (first derivative — more sensitive) Knife edge orientation determines which gradient direction is detected. Shows: shocks + boundary layers + heat plumes + mixing zones

Definition

A shock tube is another form of a blowdown or intermittent tunnel. A stagnant gas is set in motion by the passage of a strong shock wave — hence "shock tube". It generates high Mach number flow for very short durations.

Construction — Figure 11.34
  • High pressure (driver) section (HP section): gas at very high pressure (many atmospheres) — usually He or H₂ for strong shocks
  • Diaphragm: thin metal/plastic membrane separating sections
  • Low pressure (driven) section (LP section): maintained at high vacuum — contains the test gas
  • Test section: located in the LP section — model placed here
  • Automatic recording instruments and photographic equipment kept ready before test run
Working
  1. Pressure in HP section increased further until diaphragm ruptures
  2. Due to high pressure difference: strong shock wave moves into LP section at high speed, imparting high velocity (high Mach number) to test gas
  3. Simultaneously, an expansion wave propagates back into driver (HP) section
  4. A contact surface separates driver gas from test gas — they do not mix
  5. Test gas behind shock: high T and p, uniform conditions for very short duration
  6. Time of operation: in terms of milliseconds — very short
Four regions after diaphragm rupture (Fig 11.34): Region 4: Undisturbed driver gas (HP) Region 3: Driver gas processed by expansion wave Region 2: Test gas processed by shock ← uniform T₂, p₂ [TEST REGION] Region 1: Undisturbed test gas (ahead of shock) (LP) Normal shock relations at shock front: p₂/p₁ = (7M_s²-1)/6 T₂/T₁ = (7M_s²-1)(M_s²+5)/(36M_s²) [air]
Practical Problem & Advantage
Practical Problem
  • At very high vacuum in LP side: ambient air forces into section through joints and porous walls
  • Serious at high Mach numbers
  • Resolved by specially designed joints and non-porous materials
Great Advantage
  • Absence of LOW temperature in test section
  • High stagnation temperature occurs in test section
  • Representative of actual hypersonic flight conditions
  • Ideal for combustion/ignition delay studies

Subsonic Wind Tunnels (M < 1)

Used for M < 2.5 (with proper design). Main components:

  • Settling chamber: Large, low-velocity with screens and honeycombs to remove turbulence. Stagnation conditions p₀₁, T₀₁ here.
  • Contraction nozzle: Converging section (contraction ratio 6–10:1) — accelerates and uniformizes flow.
  • Test section: Uniform, parallel, subsonic flow. Model mounted here. Measurements taken.
  • Diffuser: Diverging section — decelerates flow, recovers pressure energy, reduces drive power.
  • Fan/drive: Blower type (fan pushes air in) or suction type (fan pulls air through).

Mass of air in test section ≈ 10× mass of high-pressure air. This depends on Mach number and area ratio of nozzle to test section.

§11.8.5 Supersonic Wind Tunnels

Three arrangements (§11.8.5):

  • (i) Open circuit continuous type (Fig 11.32): Compressor continuously supplies reservoir → C-D nozzle accelerates to supersonic in test section. M = f(p/p₀₁). Test section area fixed by compressor capacity and Mach number. Air must pass through drier. Compressor power very high (open-ended, wastes kinetic energy).
  • (ii) Blowdown (intermittent) type: High pressure air stored in large reservoirs. Quick opening valve starts tunnel. Operation: 1/4 to 5 min. Only power needed is to refill reservoirs — economical. Constant pressure valve at nozzle entry. Can also use high vacuum downstream (ambient air as supply).
  • (iii) Closed circuit continuous type (Fig 11.33a): Compressor + reservoir + nozzle + test section + supersonic diffuser + downstream receiver + drier + cooler. High KE at test section exit partly recovered by diffuser → compressor receives gas at higher pressure → reduced pressure ratio and power. Any gas (not just air) can be used. Stagnation temperature rises continuously — cooler needed before compressor (cooling before compression is more efficient than after).
Closed circuit compressor pressure ratio: p_r = p₀₁/p₀₂ (losses only from shock in diffuser) T₀₁ rises continuously → must cool air before compressor. Cooling before compression: W_c < W_c' (lower T_i → less work)

To reach higher Mach numbers at same flow velocity, gases of higher molecular weight can be used. Freon-21 (R = 0.095 kJ/kg·K, a = 181.4 m/s) has a sound speed ~half of air → Mach number in Freon-21 is about twice that in air at the same velocity.

Open Circuit vs Closed Circuit
Open Circuit (Continuous)
  • All air from atmosphere
  • All air must pass through drier
  • Cannot use special gases
  • Wastes kinetic energy to atmosphere
  • Lower capital cost
  • External disturbances enter
Closed Circuit (Continuous)
  • Air/gas recirculates
  • Small drier — not all air passes through
  • Any gas can be used
  • KE partly recovered by diffuser
  • Higher capital cost, cooler needed
  • Controlled, stable conditions
Special Problems of Supersonic Wind Tunnels
  1. Starting problem: Starting shock must traverse the entire test section and be swallowed by the diffuser. Diffuser throat must be large enough to pass the starting shock — otherwise tunnel won't start. Model experiences transient high loads.
  2. High power requirement: Pressure ratios of 7.8:1 (M=2) to 36:1 (M=3) require enormous compressors. Compressor must maintain p_r = p₀₁/p₀₂ continuously in closed-circuit operation.
  3. Diffuser throat oversizing: A*_diffuser must exceed A*_nozzle to start the tunnel. After starting, diffuser throat can be reduced (variable-geometry) for efficiency. Fixed-geometry tunnels accept some steady-state inefficiency.
  4. Stagnation temperature rise (closed circuit): T₀₁ continuously rises due to energy dissipation → cooler required before compressor to maintain constant T₀₁.
  5. Condensation: Rapid expansion in nozzle causes moisture condensation → use thoroughly dried air (anhydrous CaCl₂, alumina desiccant).
  6. Short run time (blowdown): 1/4 to 5 minutes — all instruments must have fast response.
  7. Model mounting: Very high dynamic pressures → rigid sting supports and force balances required.

Why Shock at Diffuser Throat is Desirable
  1. Weakest possible shock → minimum stagnation pressure loss: As supersonic flow converges in the diffuser, M → 1 at the throat. A shock at M just above 1 is extremely weak — stagnation pressure loss nearly zero. This minimises the compressor pressure ratio p_r = p₀₁/p₀₂ needed in closed-circuit tunnels.
  2. Post-shock flow immediately subsonic: After weak throat shock (M₂ ≈ 1), the diverging portion of the diffuser efficiently decelerates subsonic flow — conventional subsonic diffusion.
  3. Contrast: A shock in the test section (M = 2 or 3) would cause 28–67% stagnation pressure loss, drastically increasing required compressor work.
Stagnation pressure ratio across shock: M_shock = 1.05: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.9997 (0.03% loss) M_shock = 2.0: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.721 (28% loss) M_shock = 3.0: p₀₂/p₀₁ ≈ 0.328 (67% loss) ∴ Shock at diffuser throat (near M=1) → minimum compressor power
Why Diffuser Throat Must Be Larger Than Nozzle Throat

During start-up, the starting shock (a strong near-normal shock) must pass through the entire tunnel and be swallowed by the diffuser throat. The diffuser throat must be large enough to accommodate the mass flow with this starting shock:

For starting: A*_diffuser > A*_nozzle In steady-state operation with shock at diffuser throat: Stagnation pressure dropped across shock → p₀₂ < p₀₁ Effective throat area needed = A*_nozzle × (p₀₁/p₀₂) Since p₀₂/p₀₁ < 1 → this needed area < A*_nozzle ∴ Diffuser throat is oversized in steady state → some inefficiency Variable-geometry diffuser: throat narrowed after starting for efficiency. Fixed-geometry diffuser: accepts the steady-state inefficiency.
Key Conclusion to Write
Shock at diffuser throat is desirable because it is the weakest possible shock, giving minimum stagnation pressure loss and minimum compressor work requirement. Diffuser throat must be larger than nozzle throat to allow the strong starting shock to be swallowed during tunnel start-up; after starting, variable-geometry diffusers reduce the throat toward the ideal value for efficient steady-state operation.
