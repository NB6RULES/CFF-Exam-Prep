Normal Shocks
2.1
Why is a normal shock impossible in subsonic flow? Prove using entropy argument.
Module 2🔥 All 4 Papers3 Marks
›
Entropy Argument

A normal shock is an irreversible process — entropy must increase (Second Law). The entropy change across a normal shock is:

s₂ - s₁ = Cᵥ ln(p₂/p₁) - Cₚ ln(ρ₂/ρ₁) It can be shown from the Rankine-Hugoniot relations that for small (M₁-1): s₂ - s₁ ∝ (M₁² - 1)³ This expression is: POSITIVE when M₁ > 1 → entropy increases ✓ (physically possible) ZERO when M₁ = 1 → no shock NEGATIVE when M₁ < 1 → entropy DECREASES ✗ (violates 2nd Law)
Physical Explanation
  • In subsonic flow, pressure disturbances propagate upstream — compression waves equalize before forming a discontinuity.
  • In supersonic flow, disturbances cannot travel upstream. Waves pile up to form a shock.
  • A shock always decelerates flow: M₁ > 1 → M₂ < 1. Reverse is impossible.
Write This Line
A normal shock in subsonic flow (M₁ < 1) would require entropy to decrease, violating the Second Law of Thermodynamics. Therefore normal shocks can only exist when M₁ > 1 (supersonic upstream). Downstream flow is always subsonic (M₂ < 1).
2.2
Derive the Prandtl-Meyer relation V₁V₂ = a*² for a normal shock wave.
Module 2🔥 3/4 Papers4 Marks
›
Starting Equations
Continuity: ρ₁V₁ = ρ₂V₂ ...(1) Momentum: p₁ + ρ₁V₁² = p₂ + ρ₂V₂² ...(2) Energy: CₚT₁ + V₁²/2 = CₚT₂ + V₂²/2 = CₚT₀ ...(3)
Derivation Steps
  1. From momentum (2): (p₂-p₁) = ρ₁V₁(V₁-V₂) using continuity → p₂-p₁ = ρ₁V₁(V₁-V₂) ...(4)
  2. For perfect gas: p = ρa²/γ. Substituting: a₂²/γ - a₁²/γ = V₁V₁ - V₁V₂ - V₂V₁ + V₂² ... after algebra using (1) and (2)
  3. From energy (3): a²/(γ-1) + V²/2 = a₀²/(γ-1) = constant. At critical state (M=1): a*² = a₀² × 2/(γ+1)
  4. The energy equation gives: a₁² = a*² - (γ-1)/2 × (V₁²-a*²) × ... (critical state substitution)
  5. After substituting and eliminating a₁, a₂ using both momentum and energy:
Result
V₁ · V₂ = a*² ← Prandtl-Meyer Relation where a*² = (2/(γ+1)) × a₀² = critical speed of sound squared = (2γ/(γ+1)) × p₀/ρ₀ Physical meaning: If V₁ > a* (supersonic) → V₂ < a* (subsonic) ∴ Normal shock always converts supersonic to subsonic flow.

All Rankine-Hugoniot Relations
=== GENERAL γ === M₂² = [(γ-1)M₁² + 2] / [2γM₁² - (γ-1)] p₂/p₁ = [2γM₁² - (γ-1)] / (γ+1) T₂/T₁ = [2γM₁² - (γ-1)][2 + (γ-1)M₁²] / [(γ+1)²M₁²] ρ₂/ρ₁ = (γ+1)M₁² / [2 + (γ-1)M₁²] V₂/V₁ = [2 + (γ-1)M₁²] / [(γ+1)M₁²] === FOR AIR (γ = 1.4) — SIMPLIFIED === M₂² = (M₁² + 5) / (7M₁² - 1) p₂/p₁ = (7M₁² - 1) / 6 T₂/T₁ = (7M₁² - 1)(M₁² + 5) / (36M₁²) ρ₂/ρ₁ = 6M₁² / (M₁² + 5) Maximum density ratio as M₁→∞: ρ₂/ρ₁ → 6 for air p₀₂/p₀₁ = [6M₁²/(M₁²+5)]^3.5 × [6/(7M₁²-1)]^2.5 < 1 always

2.4
Change in entropy across a normal shock. Explain strength of a shock and variation of flow properties across it.
Module 23 Marks
›
Entropy Change
Δs = s₂ - s₁ = Cₚ ln(T₂/T₁) - R ln(p₂/p₁) = -R ln(p₀₂/p₀₁) ← since T₀ is conserved Since p₀₂/p₀₁ < 1 always: -R ln(p₀₂/p₀₁) > 0 → Δs > 0 ✓ Entropy increases across every normal shock. The stagnation pressure p₀ is the measure of irreversibility.
Strength of a Shock

Shock strength = (p₂-p₁)/p₁ = pressure ratio across shock minus 1:

Shock strength = (p₂-p₁)/p₁ = (2γ(M₁²-1))/(γ+1) For air: = (7(M₁²-1))/6 → Strength increases with M₁. At M₁=1: strength=0 (no shock). At M₁=2: strength = 2.5.
Variation of Properties Across Normal Shock
  • Pressure p: Increases (p₂ > p₁) — always
  • Temperature T: Increases (T₂ > T₁) — always
  • Density ρ: Increases (ρ₂ > ρ₁) — always, max ratio = (γ+1)/(γ-1) = 6 for air
  • Velocity V: Decreases (V₂ < V₁) — always
  • Mach number M: Decreases, M₂ < 1 always
  • Stagnation temperature T₀: UNCHANGED (adiabatic)
  • Stagnation pressure p₀: DECREASES — measure of losses
  • Entropy s: INCREASES — irreversible process
Oblique Shocks & Expansion Waves
2.5
Explain θ-β-M diagram. Define and distinguish between weak shock and strong shock solutions.
Module 2🔥 3/4 Papers4–12 Marks
›
Key Quantities
  • θ = deflection angle (flow turning, set by geometry)
  • β = wave angle (shock angle from upstream flow)
  • M₁ = upstream Mach number
Governing Equation
tan θ = 2 cot β × [M₁²sin²β - 1] / [M₁²(γ + cos 2β) + 2] For given M₁ and θ: two values of β (weak and strong shock)
Features of the θ-β-M Diagram
  1. Two solutions for any M₁ and θ < θ_max: smaller β = weak shock, larger β = strong shock.
  2. θ_max line: Maximum possible deflection for attached oblique shock. Beyond θ_max → detached bow shock.
  3. Sonic line (M₂=1): Separates weak-supersonic from weak-subsonic downstream regions.
  4. M₁=∞ limit: All curves converge, θ_max approaches ~45.5° for air.
Weak Shock
  • Smaller β (wave angle)
  • M₂ usually > 1 (supersonic)
  • Lower pressure rise
  • Lower entropy rise
  • Natural/observed solution
Strong Shock
  • Larger β (near 90°)
  • M₂ < 1 (subsonic always)
  • High pressure rise
  • High entropy rise
  • Forced by back pressure

Derive Prandtl's relation and Rankine-Hugoniot relation for an oblique shock wave.

Oblique Shock Decomposition
Decompose velocity into normal (n) and tangential (t) components: V₁ₙ = V₁ sin β V₁ₜ = V₁ cos β V₂ₙ = V₂ sin(β-θ) V₂ₜ = V₂ cos(β-θ) KEY: Tangential component UNCHANGED across oblique shock V₁ₜ = V₂ₜ (tangential momentum unaffected) Normal Mach numbers: M₁ₙ = M₁ sin β ← use this in ALL normal shock formulas M₂ₙ = M₂ sin(β-θ)
Prandtl's Relation (Oblique Shock)
V₁ₙ × V₂ₙ = a*² ← same as normal shock but using normal components
All Rankine-Hugoniot for Oblique Shock
Replace M₁ with M₁ₙ = M₁sinβ in all normal shock formulas: p₂/p₁ = (7M₁ₙ² - 1)/6 T₂/T₁ = (7M₁ₙ²-1)(M₁ₙ²+5)/(36M₁ₙ²) M₂ₙ² = (M₁ₙ²+5)/(7M₁ₙ²-1) M₂ = M₂ₙ / sin(β-θ) ← downstream Mach (full)
🔑 The entire oblique shock analysis reduces to: (1) find M₁ₙ = M₁sinβ, (2) apply normal shock formulas with M₁ₙ, (3) find M₂ = M₂ₙ/sin(β-θ).
2.7
Explain attached and detached shocks. What are reflected oblique shocks?
Module 2🔥 Jul 20233 Marks
›
Attached Shock (θ < θ_max)
  • Oblique shock anchored to tip of wedge/cone. Well-defined wave angle β.
  • Example: sharp-nosed supersonic missile, delta wing.
Detached (Bow) Shock (θ > θ_max)
  • No attached oblique shock solution exists. Curved shock stands ahead of body.
  • At centreline: nearly normal shock → subsonic downstream. Away from centreline: becomes oblique, weaker.
  • Example: blunt-nosed rockets, re-entry capsule.
Reflected Shocks

When an oblique shock hits a solid wall, it must reflect to maintain zero normal velocity at the wall. The reflected shock turns the flow back parallel to the wall.

Regular Reflection
  • θ < θ_max for M₂
  • Reflected shock is oblique
  • Two shocks + two deflections
  • Flow parallel to wall after
Mach Reflection
  • θ required > θ_max for M₂
  • Mach stem forms at wall (normal shock)
  • Triple point configuration
  • Complex flow structure

Expansion waves — Prandtl-Meyer expansion. Supersonic flow around a convex corner. Mach waves.
Module 23–4 Marks

Prandtl-Meyer Expansion

When supersonic flow encounters a convex corner (wall turns away from flow), it expands through a fan of Mach waves — isentropic, no entropy rise, stagnation pressure constant.

Prandtl-Meyer function: ν(M) = √((γ+1)/(γ-1)) arctan√((γ-1)/(γ+1)(M²-1)) - arctan√(M²-1) Turning angle: θ = ν(M₂) - ν(M₁) Given M₁ and θ: ν(M₂) = ν(M₁) + θ → look up M₂ Mach angle: μ = arcsin(1/M)
Properties Through Expansion Fan
  • Isentropic: stagnation p₀ and T₀ preserved
  • Mach number increases: M₂ > M₁
  • Pressure, temperature, density all decrease
  • Fan is bounded by Mach wave at μ₁ = arcsin(1/M₁) and Mach wave at μ₂ = arcsin(1/M₂)
Contrast with Oblique Shock
Oblique Shock (concave corner)
  • Irreversible, Δs > 0
  • p₀ decreases
  • Flow compressed, M decreases
P-M Expansion (convex corner)
  • Isentropic, Δs = 0
  • p₀ constant
  • Flow expands, M increases
